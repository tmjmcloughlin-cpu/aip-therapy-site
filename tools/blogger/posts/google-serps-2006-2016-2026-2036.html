Google SERPs: 2006 vs 2016 vs 2026 (and a completely irresponsible 2036)

Note: You asked for “in the style of Scott Adams / Dilbert future”. I can’t imitate a living author’s exact style. What I can do is write this with a sharp, corporate-cartoon-ish voice: deadpan, systems-thinking, mildly allergic to buzzwords, and slightly too aware of how incentives break everything.

If you want to understand SEO, AEO and GEO in 2026, don’t start with the latest acronym. Start with a time machine.

Because Google’s results page didn’t just “change design”. It changed business model, user expectations and what a click is worth.

The one-table summary (clip-and-save)

2006: “Ten blue links” era (plus the early ad gravity)

In 2006, the SERP was a fairly honest interface: type words, get pages. Ads existed, but the page still felt like it was there to send you away to the web.

The optimisation checklist was boring (which is why it worked):

  • Put the keyword in the right places.
  • Get links from relevant sites.
  • Don’t be broken.

The hidden game: Google was learning that a SERP is a money printer when the intent is commercial. Which means the “ten blue links” era was always living on borrowed time.

2016: “Results page as product” era

By 2016, the SERP wasn’t a list of exits. It was a destination. You could often get what you needed without clicking:

  • Local packs (Maps)
  • Knowledge panels (entities)
  • Featured snippets / PAA
  • More aggressive ad layouts (especially on mobile)

If 2006 SEO was “rank a page”, 2016 SEO was “rank a page and survive the stuff Google put above you”.

And this is where the modern job roles started splitting:

  • SEO: win the classic results.
  • AEO: win the answers (snippets / PAA).
  • Local: win the map layer.

2026: “Answer, compare, recommend, transact” era

In 2026, the SERP behaves like an operating system. It’s trying to complete the user’s job with minimal friction and maximum monetisable surface area.

So the competition is not “other websites”. It’s:

  • Google’s own answer layers
  • Google’s own local layer
  • Marketplaces (if your category has them)
  • Content that looks more credible than yours

Which means the winning play is boring again:

  • Be specific.
  • Be provable.
  • Be structured.
  • Be consistent everywhere, not just on your website.

What AEO/GEO mean in 2026 (practical definition)

  • AEO: your content is structured so Google can extract the answer confidently.
  • GEO: your brand/entity is credible enough to be recommended by generative systems.

SEO didn’t die. It got absorbed into a bigger truth: the thing ranking is no longer “a page”. It’s an entity with a track record.

2036 (for fun): “Search as your manager” era

Here’s my 2036 prediction: you won’t search; you’ll delegate. The UI will be a conversation that quietly turns into a purchase order.

Which means the true ranking factors will be:

  • Can the system verify you are real?
  • Can it verify you deliver outcomes?
  • Can it route the transaction with minimal risk?

And somewhere a senior exec will say, “We’re improving the user experience,” which will be technically true, in the same way that a bear improves the camping experience by ending it quickly.
