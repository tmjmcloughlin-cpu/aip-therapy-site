What is EMDR therapy?

EMDR — Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing — is a structured, evidence-based therapy recognised by NICE and the World Health Organisation as a first-line treatment for PTSD and trauma.

When something traumatic happens, the brain sometimes fails to process the memory properly. Instead of being stored as a past event, it stays raw — triggering the same emotional and physical responses as if it were happening now. EMDR uses bilateral stimulation (typically guided eye movements) to help the brain finally complete that processing.

The result is not forgetting what happened. It is that the memory loses its charge — it becomes something that happened, rather than something that is still happening.

How EMDR therapy works at AIP

1

Assessment & preparation

Before any trauma processing begins, James takes time to understand your history, identify the memories causing distress, and ensure you have the resources and stability to engage with the work safely. There is no rushing this stage.

2

Reprocessing

Using bilateral stimulation — guided eye movements or tapping — James works with you to process the targeted memories. You remain fully in control throughout. The memory does not disappear; it loses its power to destabilise you.

3

Integration & consolidation

As processing progresses, James integrates EMDR with hypnotherapy and psychotherapeutic techniques to consolidate the gains — building a more stable, grounded sense of self that holds between sessions and beyond.

EMDR therapy — frequently asked questions

What is EMDR therapy?

EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing) is a structured therapy that helps the brain reprocess traumatic memories that have become stuck. It uses bilateral stimulation — typically eye movements — to allow the brain to process distressing memories in a way that reduces their emotional charge. Recognised by NICE and the WHO for PTSD.

What can EMDR help with?

EMDR is highly effective for PTSD, complex trauma, childhood abuse, accidents, military and combat trauma, emergency services trauma, grief, phobias and anxiety rooted in past experience.
